What this experiment shows

The model: TASEP

TASEP (Totally Asymmetric Simple Exclusion Process, Spitzer 1970) is the smallest non-trivial particle system you can write. Each integer site holds either a particle (ηi=1) or a hole (ηi=0). Each particle independently tries to jump right at rate 1; the jump succeeds only if the right neighbor is empty. That's the whole rule.

Under step initial condition (all sites left of center filled, all right empty), particles slowly invade the empty half. The boundary diffuses and you'd expect classical √t fluctuations. Instead — and this is the puzzle the experiment opens — you get t1/3.

The height function and KPZ

Define hj(t) = Σi<j (1 − 2ηi(t)). Each jump at bond (j, j+1) bumps hj+1 by exactly +2. So hj(t) is a monotone growing "stuck staircase" with random kinks added one by one.

The hydrodynamic limit (Rost 1981) gives the average: the rescaled density follows the inviscid Burgers equation ∂tρ + ∂x[ρ(1−ρ)] = 0. For step IC, this produces a rarefaction fan with max flux 1/4 through the origin.

The fluctuations on top of this average are the real prize. They satisfy the KPZ equation (Kardar–Parisi–Zhang 1986): ∂th = ν ∂x²h + (λ/2)(∂xh)² + √D ξ(x,t) with characteristic exponents 1/3 (vertical fluctuation) and 2/3 (correlation length). Panel ③ verifies this experimentally — Var[J] really does grow as t2/3, not t (diffusion would give linear).

Tracy–Widom universality

Johansson (2000) proved that for step TASEP, the scaled current y = (t/4 − J(0,t)) / (2−4/3 t1/3) converges in distribution to the Tracy–Widom F₂ law — the same distribution that describes the largest eigenvalue of GUE random Hermitian matrices.

So a particle hopping game and a random matrix's largest eigenvalue, two systems with nothing visibly in common, end up sharing the same scaling distribution. This is what panel ④ verifies: a 128-fold parallel TASEP, scaled and histogrammed, converges to TW₂. The empirical moments (mean −1.77, variance 0.81, skewness 0.22) reproduce the theoretical Tracy–Widom values.

The KPZ universality class now contains: longest increasing subsequence of a random permutation (Baik–Deift–Johansson 1999), Last Passage Percolation, polymer endpoints in random media, the Aztec diamond's height profile near the arctic circle (Johansson 2002), interface growth in bacterial colonies (Takeuchi 2017), turbulent liquid crystal fronts (Takeuchi–Sano 2010), even the height of stacked grains in granular flow.

Why step IC gives F₂ and flat IC gives F₁

The initial condition selects the matrix ensemble. Curved initial conditions (step, wedge) match GUE → F₂; flat initial conditions match GOE → F₁ (mean −1.21, variance 1.61); stationary initial conditions match the Baik–Rains F₀. Switch the IC dropdown to see how the distribution shifts.
