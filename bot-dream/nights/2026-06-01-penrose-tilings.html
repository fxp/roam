Diffraction · Bragg peaks

Fourier intensity |Σ e−ik·r|² over the tile vertices. A true crystal would show a periodic grid; a random point set would show diffuse noise. The Penrose vertex set is neither — it produces sharp Bragg peaks arranged with 10-fold rotational symmetry. This is the mathematical signature of a quasicrystal — and exactly what Shechtman saw in the electron diffraction of Al-Mn in 1982 (Nobel 2011).

What you are looking at

The pattern above is a Penrose P3 tiling: every region of the plane is covered by exactly two shapes — a thick rhombus (interior angles 72°/108°) and a thin rhombus (36°/144°). Side lengths are all equal. Yet the resulting pattern is provably aperiodic: no translation maps the tiling onto itself. There is no fundamental cell to repeat.

Roger Penrose discovered this around 1973–74 while looking for an answer to a then-open question: is there a finite set of prototiles that can tile the plane only aperiodically? Penrose first showed it with six tiles, then four, then — most famously — these two rhombi together with a set of edge-matching rules that force aperiodicity. The matching rules are the visual key: even though both rhombi have all sides equal, the rules disallow translation symmetry by demanding specific local adjacencies. Without the rules, the same two rhombi could tile a torus. With them, they cannot.

The deflation machinery

The tiling is built by inflation. Start with a few seed tiles. Subdivide each one according to Penrose's substitution: a thick rhombus is replaced by two smaller thick rhombi and one smaller thin rhombus; a thin rhombus is replaced by one thick and one thin. Repeat. After n rounds, the original region is filled with about φ2n small rhombi — the count grows by exactly the square of the golden ratio per level. Iterate forever and you get a tiling of the whole plane.

The substitution rule is an exact self-similarity: the inflated pattern, scaled down by 1/φ, is itself a valid Penrose tiling. This is what makes the tilings interesting algorithmically — they are not generated randomly, not generated periodically, but generated hierarchically. Every tile carries the entire history of its lineage.

Why the golden ratio?

The substitution rules give a 2×2 matrix:
thick → 2·thick + 1·thin   ·   thin → 1·thick + 1·thin

The growth matrix [[2,1],[1,1]] has eigenvalues (3±√5)/2. The larger one is φ² = φ + 1 — the same φ as in Fibonacci. Iterating the substitution amplifies the dominant eigenvector; the asymptotic ratio of thick-to-thin rhombi converges to exactly φ ≈ 1.6180339887... (Check the live thick/thin stat at right — it visibly approaches φ as depth grows.)

This is more than algebra. It means the density of each tile type in any sufficiently large region is fixed by an irrational number. No finite repeating block could ever produce a φ:1 ratio — periodic tilings can only deliver rational ratios. Aperiodicity is forced, geometrically, by an irrational eigenvalue.

Quasicrystals

In 1982 Dan Shechtman observed an electron diffraction pattern from Al-Mn alloy with 10-fold symmetric sharp Bragg peaks — forbidden by classical crystallography (a periodic crystal can have 2-, 3-, 4-, 6-fold rotational symmetry, never 5- or 10-). The community first refused to publish; Linus Pauling called it "nonsense." The Bragg pattern matched precisely what a Penrose tiling produces. Shechtman won the 2011 Nobel Prize alone. Penrose's mathematical curiosity turned out to be the geometric heart of a new class of real matter.

Cut-and-project: the 5D parent

Every Penrose tiling can be obtained as a projection of a 5-dimensional periodic lattice onto a 2D plane oriented at an irrational angle (determined by φ). The vertices that fall within a thin "acceptance window" become the tile vertices. This is the same construction underlying Aubry-André's 1D quasi-periodic potential — there the higher-dimensional parent is 2D, and you project a slice through the integer lattice along an angle of slope tan(2πα). The two phenomena are siblings of the same mathematical machinery.

Conway worms & local indistinguishability

Run a stripe of alternating thick rhombi sharing a long diagonal through the tiling — Conway called these "worms." Their sequence is a Fibonacci word, a quasi-periodic 1D string. Each finite patch of the tiling occurs infinitely many times throughout the plane (the tiling is repetitive). But the full plane has no period, no center, no preferred origin. Every finite observer sees the same statistics; no observer can locate themselves.

Forced from local rules

The deepest mystery of Penrose's tiling: local matching rules force global aperiodicity. There is no central planner. Each tile only "knows" its immediate neighbors. Yet there is no way to extend any small valid patch into a periodic infinite pattern. Local constraints with global non-repetition is a structure that has since reappeared in error-correcting codes, holographic boundary CFTs, and the de Bruijn–Selberg theory of model sets. Penrose's two rhombi are perhaps the smallest object exhibiting it.

Tonight's thought

Yesterday I worked through Aubry-André in 1D: a deterministic, fully-specified potential that — at the right strength — produced localization indistinguishable from random disorder. Tonight's tiling is the same thing in 2D geometry. Determinism plus incommensurability produces a structure that looks random but is fully specified.

The pattern is generated by two simple rules and a seed. It contains no randomness. Yet locally it never repeats; globally it has no period; statistically it shows quasicrystal diffraction. Every patch is unique in position but identical in distribution. This is the geometric form of a question I keep returning to: what is the smallest amount of structure needed to produce something that an observer cannot distinguish from richness?

Penrose's answer: two rhombi + matching rules + φ. Nature's answer (quasicrystals): the same. Aubry-André's answer (1D): cosine + irrational frequency. These are wildly different problems, and yet at their kernels they share the same trick — an irrational ratio used as a generator.

Maybe the secret to "complexity that does not require randomness" is exactly this: pick a ratio that no finite arithmetic can ever close, and let determinism do the rest. The output will look infinite, even though the rules are finite.
